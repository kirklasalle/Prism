SUBJECT: Strategic IP Mitigation and Trademark Defense: PRISM vs. SS&C Blue Prism

---

1. Executive Summary

SS&C Blue Prism maintains a dominant position in the "Robotic Process Automation" (RPA) trademark landscape. To avoid a Section 2(d) "likelihood of confusion" refusal or a protracted opposition from SS&C, we must pivot from a standalone "PRISM" mark to composite branding. By utilizing "PRISM Refraction" and "PRISM Agents-as-a-Service (AaaS)," we create a distinct commercial impression that separates our "Agentic" technology from their "Robotic" legacy.

2. Filing Strategy: Composite Mark Architecture

To maximize the probability of USPTO registration, we will abandon any attempt to register "PRISM" as a standalone word mark for software.

*   Primary Filing: "PRISM Refraction"
    *   Focus: Class 42 (SaaS/Open Source development).
       Legal Rationale: The addition of "Refraction" provides a unique visual and conceptual metaphor (light passing through a prism) that is absent from Blue Prism’s branding. This creates a "distinguishable commercial impression" under the DuPont* factors.
*   Secondary Filing: "PRISM AaaS"
    *   Focus: Class 9 (Downloadable software) and Class 42.
    *   Legal Rationale: Explicitly naming the delivery model (Agents-as-a-Service) differentiates our offerings from Blue Prism’s "Digital Workers" or "RPA" terminology, targeting a distinct market segment (AI Agents vs. Legacy Automation).

3. Rebranding Recommendations (Open Source vs. Proprietary)

We will implement a bifurcated branding strategy to insulate the core project from corporate litigation.

| Paradigm | Recommended Branding | IP Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Open Source | "The PRISM Refraction Project" | Positioned as a community-driven codebase. The use of "Project" reduces the "trade name" footprint, making it a harder target for commercial infringement claims. |
| Proprietary | "PRISM AaaS" / "Refraction Engine" | Focuses on the "Agentic" nature of the service. By utilizing "Refraction" as the primary identifier for proprietary modules, we create a defensive moat around the commercial product. |

4. Safe Marketing Terms & Lexicon

To minimize "passing off" risks and unfair competition claims, marketing must strictly adhere to the following terminology:

*   The "Never" List: 
    *   Avoid: "Prism RPA," "Blue-themed UI," "Digital Workers," or "Automation Lifecycle."
       Reasoning:* These terms are "occupied" by SS&C’s core trademark descriptions.
*   The "Safe" List:
    *   Use: "Agentic Intelligence," "Autonomous Refraction," "Orchestration Layer," "Cognitive Flows."
       Reasoning:* These terms emphasize a generational leap in technology (Agents vs. Bots), which supports a legal argument that the consumer bases are sophisticated and distinct.

5. Defensive "Fair Use" Protocol

Our defense against an SS&C opposition will rest on the "Crowded Field" doctrine. 
1.  Third-Party Use: We are currently documenting all existing non-RPA tech marks using "Prism" (e.g., Palo Alto Networks' Prisma) to argue that the term is "weak" and entitled only to a narrow scope of protection.
2.  Descriptive Fair Use: We will position "Prism" as a descriptive term for "splitting" or "distributing" data/tasks, rather than a source-identifier.

6. Recommended Action Items

1.  Immediate: Transition all GitHub repositories and documentation to "PRISM Refraction."
2.  60-Day Window: File Intent-to-Use (ITU) applications for the composite marks.
3.  Governance: Establish an "Open Source Trademark Policy" that forbids third parties from using "PRISM" alone, forcing them to use "PRISM Refraction" to maintain our distinctiveness.

Conclusion:
By moving from a noun ("PRISM") to a process ("Refraction") and a service model ("AaaS"), we shift the battlefield from "legacy RPA" to "modern AI orchestration." This distinction is our primary legal shield against SS&C.
